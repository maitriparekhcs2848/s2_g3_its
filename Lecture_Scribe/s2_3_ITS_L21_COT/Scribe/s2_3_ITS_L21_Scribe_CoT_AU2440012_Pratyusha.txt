\section*{Lecture 21 \& 22: Inequalities and Tail Bounds}
(CSE400 – Fundamentals of Probability in Computing)

\section*{1. Motivation: Tail Bounds}

The lecture begins by introducing the idea of tail bounds.

\textbf{Key Idea:}

We are interested in probabilities of the form:

\[
P(X \ge a)
\]

where $a$ is far from the mean.  
These probabilities correspond to the tail of a distribution.

\textbf{Interpretation:}

The “tail” represents rare or extreme events.  
Example: Getting unusually many heads in coin tosses.

\textbf{Running Example (Used Throughout the Lecture)}

\textbf{Experiment:}  
Flip a fair coin $n$ times.

\textbf{Random Variable:}  
$X =$ number of heads.

\textbf{Distribution:}

\[
X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, \frac{1}{2})
\]

\textbf{Mean:}

\[
E[X] = \frac{n}{2}
\]

\textbf{Question:}

\[
P\left(X \ge \frac{3n}{4}\right)
\]

\textbf{Interpretation:}  
This asks: What is the probability of getting significantly more heads than expected?  
Since the mean is $n/2$, the value $3n/4$ lies in the right tail.

\textbf{Important Observation:}

We will not compute exact probabilities.  
Instead, we will compute upper bounds.  
These bounds will become progressively tighter using different inequalities.

\section*{2. Markov’s Inequality}

\textbf{Definition}

Let $X$ be a non-negative random variable.

Then for any $a > 0$:

\[
P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}
\]

\textbf{Assumptions}

$X \ge 0$  
$E[X]$ exists

\textbf{Interpretation}

The probability that $X$ is large is bounded by its mean scaled by $a$.  
This is a very general inequality (works for any distribution).

\textbf{Example: Exam Grades}

\textbf{Problem Statement:}  
Average grade = 70\%  
“A” cutoff = 90\%  
Find an upper bound on:

\[
P(X \ge 90)
\]

\textbf{Step-by-Step Solution}

\textbf{Step 1: Define Random Variable}

Let $X =$ student grade

\textbf{Step 2: Identify Mean}

\[
\mu = E[X] = 70
\]

\textbf{Step 3: Apply Markov’s Inequality}

\[
P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}
\]

\textbf{Step 4: Substitute Values}

\[
P(X \ge 90) \le \frac{70}{90}
\]

\textbf{Step 5: Simplify}

\[
P(X \ge 90) \le \frac{7}{9}
\]

\[
P(X \ge 90) \le 0.778
\]

\textbf{Final Result}

At most 77.8\% of students can receive an A.

\textbf{Key Insight:}  
This bound is valid but loose.  
It does not use any information beyond the mean.

\section*{3. Chebyshev’s Inequality}

\textbf{Motivation}

Markov’s inequality only uses the mean.  
To get tighter bounds, we incorporate variance.

\textbf{Definition}

For any random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

\[
P(|X - \mu| \ge a) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{a^2}
\]

\textbf{Interpretation}

Measures how far $X$ deviates from its mean.  
Provides a bound on deviation probability.

\textbf{Rewriting for One-Sided Tail}

\[
P(X \ge \mu + a) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{a^2}
\]

\textbf{Key Insight}

Uses both:

Mean $\mu$  
Variance $\sigma^2$

Produces a tighter bound than Markov.

\section*{4. Chernoff Bound}

\textbf{Motivation}

Even Chebyshev bounds can be loose.  
We want exponentially decreasing bounds.

\textbf{Idea}

Transform the random variable using exponentials.  
Apply Markov’s inequality on transformed variable.

\textbf{Form (General Idea)}

For suitable $t > 0$:

\[
P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}}
\]

\textbf{Key Insight}

This introduces:

Moment Generating Function (MGF)  
Leads to very tight bounds.

\textbf{For Binomial Distribution}

Chernoff bounds give results of the form:

\[
P(X \ge (1 + \delta)\mu) \le e^{-c\mu}
\]

\textbf{Interpretation:}  
Probability of large deviation drops exponentially.

\section*{5. Poisson Tail}

\textbf{Context}

Similar tail bounds apply to Poisson distributions.

\textbf{Key Idea}

Tail probabilities can also be bounded using exponential techniques.

\section*{6. Jensen’s Inequality}

\textbf{Definition}

If $f$ is a convex function, then:

\[
f(E[X]) \le E[f(X)]
\]

\textbf{Convex Function Condition}

A function $f$ is convex if:

\[
f(\lambda x_1 + (1 - \lambda)x_2) \le \lambda f(x_1) + (1 - \lambda)f(x_2)
\]

\textbf{Interpretation}

Applying function after expectation gives lower value than expectation after function.

\textbf{Importance}

Used in:

Deriving Chernoff bounds  
Bounding expectations

\section*{7. Case Study: System-Level Understanding}

\textbf{Problem}

How to predict worst-case demand on a server?

\textbf{Connection to Tail Bounds}

Server load can be modeled as a random variable.  
We want:

\[
P(\text{load} \ge \text{capacity})
\]

\textbf{Use of Inequalities}

Markov $\rightarrow$ rough estimate  
Chebyshev $\rightarrow$ better estimate  
Chernoff $\rightarrow$ tight estimate

\section*{Conclusion}

Tail bounds help ensure:

Reliability  
System safety  
Performance guarantees

\section*{Final Summary}

\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
\hline
Inequality & Uses & Strength \\
\hline
Markov & Mean only & Weak \\
Chebyshev & Mean + Variance & Moderate \\
Chernoff & Exponential moments & Strong \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\section*{Key Takeaways}

1. Tail bounds estimate probabilities of rare events. \\
2. Markov inequality provides a basic upper bound. \\
3. Chebyshev improves it using variance. \\
4. Chernoff bounds give exponentially tight bounds. \\
5. Jensen’s inequality supports advanced derivations. \\
6. These tools are essential for analyzing worst-case scenarios.
